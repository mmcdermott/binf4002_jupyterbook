# Study Guides

These are short, structured study guides — one per lecture — designed for the way most students actually review:

- **Key terms** — crisp, ML-precise definitions of the vocabulary you should be able to recognize and reproduce. If a term in the table is one you can't define in a sentence, that's where to focus.
- **Core insights** — the load-bearing claims of the lecture, in callout-box form. The thing you should be able to *say* in your own words after reviewing.
- **Self-check questions** — short prompts you should be able to answer without looking. If you can answer all of them, you have a working understanding.
- **Clinical context** (where applicable) — short notes on how the lecture's machinery shows up in real clinical decision-making.
- **Additional resources** — curated, mostly free, mostly online. Use these to go deeper on whichever piece of the lecture is shakiest.

Lectures 1-10 (and the neural-network supplement) study guides were written first; lectures 11-27 follow the same format. Lecture 28 is a course-wide recap and doesn't have its own study guide — re-read the [concept map](../concept_map.md) instead.

## How to use these

- **Active recall first, lookup second.** Don't read the key-terms table top-to-bottom. Cover the right column with your hand, look at each term, and try to give the definition before peeking. The terms you struggle with are the ones to study.
- **Self-check questions are not multiple choice.** They are open-ended on purpose. Write your answer down (in a notebook or a scratch file), *then* check.
- **Use additional resources strategically.** Each is annotated with what it does best. If a topic is shaky, pick the one most aligned to your learning style — visual (3Blue1Brown, StatQuest), formal (Bishop, Murphy), or applied (Karpathy, fast.ai, Coursera).
