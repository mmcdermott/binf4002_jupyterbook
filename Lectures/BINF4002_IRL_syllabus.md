# Course-level arc

BINF 4002 builds from mathematical and statistical foundations toward practical health AI. The first arc establishes the language of vectors, probability, optimization, risk, and empirical learning. The second arc turns that language into model training and evaluation: binary classification, decision rules, losses, overfitting, regularization, inductive bias, and generalization. The third arc introduces modern neural and foundation-model methods, culminating in LLMs and the question of what “foundation model” should mean in health.

The second half applies this machinery to health and biomedical settings. It moves modality by modality: EHR and claims data, clinical text, clinical NLP, medical imaging, population health/survival data, causality/fairness, genomics, proteins/small molecules, modern biological AI, and deployment. A recurring pattern is used across modalities:

1. Map the data modality onto the ML taxonomy students already know.
2. Identify where that mapping breaks in health or biomedicine.
3. Explain what that breakage does to task definition, representation, modeling, evaluation, and deployment.

The result is a course about **why ML for health is not just generic ML on health-shaped inputs**. The central course theme is that health data are generated by systems of care, measurement, biology, incentives, and access. Models inherit those structures.

# Cross-lecture concept map

## Foundations → ML training

- **Linear algebra (L2)** provides the language of vectors, matrices, bases, linear operators, and representations.
- **Probability (L3-L4)** provides random variables, joint distributions, conditioning, Bayes’ rule, expectation, and statistical inference.
- **Calculus and optimization (L5-L6)** turn functions and losses into objects that can be minimized, first deterministically and then in expectation over data-generating distributions.
- **Probabilistic modeling (L7)** connects likelihood, negative log likelihood, latent structure, and optimization.
- **Evaluation and training (L8-L10)** connect learned scores to clinical decisions, performance metrics, calibration, model capacity, overfitting, regularization, and generalization.

## ML fundamentals → modern AI

- **Neural networks** generalize the optimization arc to expressive, differentiable, parametric function classes.
- **LLMs** instantiate next-token prediction as a universal-seeming interface for language tasks, connecting probabilistic modeling to transformers and attention.
- **Foundation models** generalize from single-task prediction to models conditioned on a task description, with special emphasis on when task conditioning, data efficiency, and zero-/few-shot behavior matter.

## Modern AI → health data modalities

- **EHR/claims data (L17-L18)** introduce the core problem of health event streams: irregular timing, informative missingness, selection, coding systems, cohort definitions, and task-window alignment.
- **Clinical text and NLP (L19-L20)** show that language in health is domain-specific, semi-structured, abbreviation-heavy, negation-heavy, and constrained by annotation and privacy.
- **Medical imaging (L21-L22)** extends the modality arc to arrays, volumes, multi-scale images, DICOM metadata, transfer learning, segmentation, and site generalization.

## Health data → population, causality, fairness, and deployment

- **Population health and survival analysis (L23)** broaden the data lens beyond EHRs to registries, surveys, biobanks, trials, censoring, competing risks, Kaplan-Meier, Cox models, and modern survival models.
- **Causality and fairness (L24)** formalize the difference between prediction and intervention, introduce DAGs and propensity scores, and connect fairness metrics to causal mechanisms and subgroup evaluation.
- **Deployment (L28)** closes the course by showing how drift, feedback loops, label delay, alert fatigue, regulation, and differential access alter model behavior after validation.

## Biomedical molecules and modern biological AI

- **DNA/genetics/gene regulation (L25)** maps genotype, expression, population structure, GWAS, PRS, and regulation to ML-relevant representations and confounding structures.
- **Proteins/molecules/structural biology (L26)** introduces classical baselines for sequences, structures, fingerprints, docking, QSAR, and drug discovery.
- **Modern biological AI (L27)** introduces protein language models, AlphaFold-style structure prediction, genomic foundation models, equivariant 3D molecular models, and generative protein design.

**Key learning objectives.**

**Key takeaways.**

- A random variable is not merely a random number; it is a function defined over an underlying probability space.
- Probability measures assign mass to events, and events are structured subsets of possible samples.
- Expectation is the bridge between random processes and objectives/losses.

**Main points covered.**

- Probability triples: sample space, event set, probability measure.
- Discrete probability examples.
- Random variables and induced distributions.
- Expectation.
- Joint random variables and the move from single-variable uncertainty to relationships among variables.

**Connections.**

- Supports Lecture 4’s conditionals, Bayes rule, and information theory.

## Lecture 6 — Probabilistic optimization: optimizing expected performance

**Actually covered.** The lecture moved from deterministic optimization to probabilistic optimization, where inputs and labels are random variables and the objective is performance in expectation over a data-generating distribution.

**Key learning objectives.**

- Distinguish deterministic objectives from probabilistic objectives.
- Understand expected loss as the natural target when data are sampled.
- Understand why training data are samples from a larger distribution.
- Recognize the gap between empirical optimization and expected/deployment performance.
- Understand why iid assumptions and future samples matter.

**Key takeaways.**

- In ML, the function being optimized is usually only observed through sampled data.
- The real goal is not merely minimizing loss on the observed sample, but minimizing expected loss on future samples.
- This creates the central train/test/generalization problem.

**Main points covered.**

- Deterministic optimization with fixed inputs.
- Random variables for inputs and labels.
- Expected objective functions.
- Empirical data as samples from a joint distribution.
- Static versus sampled/probabilistic functions.
- Implications for generalization and future data.

**Connections.**

- Forms the bridge from calculus/optimization to ML training.
- Prepares students for Lecture 7’s likelihood framework and Lecture 9’s binary classifier training pipeline.

## Included unnumbered lecture — Foundation models

**Actually covered.** This lecture examined what “foundation model” means, especially in health. It contrasted classical single-task models, multi-task models, few-shot/transfer-learning models, and zero-shot/task-conditioned models. It emphasized ambiguity in the term and proposed a task-conditioned view: a model taking both input `x` and task `alpha`.

**Key learning objectives.**

- Understand the field’s broad definition of foundation models as broad-data, self-supervised, adaptable models.
- Understand why the term “foundation model” is ambiguous.
- Distinguish single-task, multi-task, few-shot/transfer, and zero-shot/task-conditioned behavior.
- Understand task conditioning as a formal lens for foundation models.
- Understand why foundation models may improve task-specific data efficiency but not necessarily total data efficiency.
- Evaluate when foundation models are useful in health and what task distribution they cover.
- Understand why EHR autoregressive models can be viewed as foundation models under a universal-task framing.

**Key takeaways.**

- Always ask: “foundation model over what domain and what task distribution?”
- Foundation models are not automatically better than optimized single-task models on a fixed task.
- Their distinctive value is often data-efficient adaptation, task accessibility, and broad reuse.
- Evaluation is harder because it is always evaluation over some distribution of tasks.
- In health, ambiguity about tasks, labels, and deployment contexts makes foundation-model claims especially easy to overstate.

**Main points covered.**

- Motivation using clinical question-answer examples from the previous LLM lecture.
- Definition from the foundation-model literature.
- Spectrum from classical single-task models to zero-shot models.
- Multi-task, few-shot, transfer-learning, and zero-shot distinctions.
- Formal framing with task `alpha` as part of the input.
- Task distributions and inductive bias.
- Labeled data efficiency versus total data efficiency.
- Evaluation over task distributions.
- EHR autoregressive modeling as a possible universal-task foundation-model setup.
- Prior-fitted networks and extensional task inputs as another foundation-model example.

**Connections.**

- Connects LLM prompting to health-specific models.
- Prepares for EHR sequence modeling, clinical NLP, protein language models, genomic FMs, and modern biological AI.

# What students should be able to integrate by the end

By the end of the course, students should be able to reason across the full lifecycle of health AI:

1. **Mathematical formulation.** Express inputs, outputs, losses, probabilities, and optimization problems clearly.
2. **Model training.** Connect model class, objective, optimization, regularization, and evaluation.
3. **Metric interpretation.** Interpret calibration, discrimination, utility, PPV, AUROC, and task-specific metrics.
4. **Representation choice.** Translate EHR events, clinical text, images, genetic data, proteins, and molecules into ML representations while naming what is lost.
5. **Data-generating process.** Identify how care delivery, billing, measurement, biology, selection, and access shape observed data.
6. **Task definition.** Align prediction time, label windows, censoring, competing risks, and actionability.
7. **Causal and fairness reasoning.** Distinguish prediction from intervention and evaluate fairness claims mechanistically.
8. **Foundation-model claims.** Ask what domain and task distribution a foundation model actually covers, and whether it improves task-specific data efficiency, total data efficiency, accessibility, or something else.
9. **Deployment reasoning.** Anticipate drift, delayed labels, feedback loops, alert fatigue, differential deployment, and regulation.
10. **Scientific skepticism.** Treat benchmarks, data sources, model performance, and deployment claims as conditional on assumptions that must be made explicit.

# Major through-lines to emphasize in student reflection

## 1. The model is never separate from the task

From Lecture 8 onward, the course repeatedly shows that the same score can support different decisions depending on thresholds, utilities, calibration, prevalence, and workflow. This principle becomes stronger in health: task definitions require time windows, censoring assumptions, actionability, and clinical context.

## 2. Data are generated, not given

EHRs, claims, notes, images, registries, trials, genetic cohorts, and molecular assays are all products of measurement systems. Students should ask who was measured, why, when, with what technology, under what incentives, and with what missingness.

## 3. Representation is a scientific claim

Tabularizing an EHR, chunking a sequence, windowing a CT, tokenizing a note, embedding a protein, fingerprinting a molecule, or representing a genomic region all impose assumptions. These assumptions determine what the model can and cannot learn.

## 4. Generalization is contextual

Generalization is introduced mathematically through probabilistic optimization and empirically through training/evaluation, but in health it becomes a question about sites, populations, workflows, time, disease prevalence, coding practices, scanners, interventions, and deployment feedback.

## 5. Modern AI does not remove classical baselines

The course repeatedly returns to classical methods: linear algebra, Bayes, likelihoods, kNN, calibration, Cox models, sequence alignment, MSA, PSSMs, homology modeling, QSAR, docking, and diagnostic metrics. Modern models should be interpreted as extensions or alternatives, not replacements for understanding.

## 6. Deployment creates new distributions

The final lecture makes explicit what earlier lectures imply: a validated model enters a world where clinicians respond, populations shift, labels arrive late, alerts create fatigue, access is unequal, and regulatory rules constrain updates. Post-deployment data are not passive observations; they are partly consequences of the model itself.
